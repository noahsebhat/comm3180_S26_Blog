Political speeches, marketing reports, and news headlines often refer to the “average American”. But in a country as large and diverse as the United States, it’s not immediately clear who that person is or what they’re actually like. Using publicly available personality survey data, I tried to figure it out and piece together a portrait of this frequently mentioned but rarely defined figure.

First, I wanted to understand the demographics of the average American: their age, race, and gender. According to the data, the average American is a 21 year old White female.

But what is this person like? The personality survey scores respondents across five traits: openness, conscientiousness, extroversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism, giving them a percentile score for each. Calculating the median for each trait, I found that the average Americans falls into the 52nd percentile for openness, 52nd for conscientousness, 49th for extroversion, 54th for agreeableness, 52nd for conscientiousness, and 54th for neuroticism.

While these results help shed light on the identity of the average American, they may be more of an abstraction rather than an accurate representation. To determine how representative this profile really is, I measured how far each person’s personality differs from the median across the five traits. On average, respondents differ from the median profile by 25.4 percentile points per trait!

In other words, the average American isn’t very average at all. Part of the reason may be that personality shifts over time, meaning there isn’t one typical profile for all ages. To test this, I calculated the average personality profiles across different age groups: under 18, 18-24, 25-34, 35-49, and 50+.

Unsurprisingly, personality varies noticeably across age groups, with older Americans scoring higher across almost every trait. The biggest changes appear in conscientiousness and agreeableness, which both rise sharply with age, suggesting that Americans tend to become more responsible and socially cooperative over time.

In the end, the “average American” turns out to be more of a statistical amalgamation rather a true representation. While some people come close to or maybe even match this profile, most differ from it significantly, and what counts as “average” changes across age. In a country as diverse as the US, no single identity can capture everybody. And that’s okay!
